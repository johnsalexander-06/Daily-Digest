ESET found 11 unrevoked UEFI shims — some from 2013 — that let attackers bypass Secure Boot

Researchers at ESET identified 11 old UEFI shims, several dating back to 2013, that Microsoft never revoked, leaving a working path to bypass Secure Boot on affected systems. Because the shims are signed and trusted, the bypass defeats the boot-integrity control that most endpoint security models assume is intact underneath them. So WhatBoot-level trust is the assumption your entire endpoint security stack rests on, and it has been quietly false on some hardware for years. Have your cyber staff confirm the revocation status of shims across your fielded platforms — reimaging is not a remediation for this class of compromise.

The self-taught engineer whose wing carried the B-24 — and 18,482 bombers with it

The most-produced American military aircraft of World War II was not a fighter or a trainer but the B-24 Liberator, a complex four-engine heavy bomber that American industry turned out 18,482 times in five years and flew in every theater of the war. What made it possible was its wing — the Davis wing, the product of a self-made engineer's bet on an airfoil design the establishment had no particular reason to trust. Without it the Liberator would not have had its range and load, and might never have existed at all. It's a reminder that the workhorse of a war can hinge on one person's conviction about a curve.
